As part of an ongoing research on Computational Thinking, I was able to listen to Dr. Jeannette Wing's CSTA talk on the concepts, present, and future of CT. Here are some of my reflections on her discussion.

- It was interesting how Dr. Wing considered computers to be either machines or humans - this means that she regards humans as just another form of computers that can carry out algorithms and programs. I especially enjoyed her comparison of instructions for baking chocolate chip cookies to a computer program.

- Dr. Wing emphasized how software is the key factor that differentiates CT from math or engineering, in that CT is not bound by laws of physics or nature. Her quote - "Creativity is only limited by human thought" - was especially captivating in that it reflected the very reason I myself took up computer science in the first place.

- While she was pleasantly surprised by how CT has garnered a wide audience, the problems that follow - namely, the lack of foundation on what concepts should be taught to certain age groups - are issues that should be dealt by researchers. Her example of recursion was interesting in that even college-level students have a hard time grasping how it works, much less elementary school students.

- Following from the above bullet point, I was surprised to learn that the education side of CT is the main field of research in the status quo. It seems that the curriculum has been established to a certain point, but researchers are still working on when each concept should be taught to students.

- Though computers are becoming more widely distributed across the world, students are not really interested in how computers actually work. Instead of asking how to use a certain feature, the ideal goal would be to have students ask questions such as: "How does everything fit inside this computer?", which would lead to discussions on important CT concepts including compression and digitization.
